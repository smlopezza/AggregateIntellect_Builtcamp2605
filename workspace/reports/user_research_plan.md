# User Research Plan

## quality_risk_focus_for_user_research

### current_quality_risk

Three co-priority quality risks are driving user research. Two were identified and partially validated in the evaluation phase (R1, R7); one emerged from reflection after evaluation (R8).

**R1 — Multi-connection misclassification**
When a contact has multiple valid connection points (e.g., shared professional journey AND shared cultural identity), the navigator fails to identify and lead with the highest-leverage angle. It either blends both weakly, picks the less strategically relevant one, or treats them as equal weight. The newcomer walks into the coffee chat with a diluted prep strategy.

**R7 — Relationship depth blindness**
The navigator generates high-quality prep for individual coffee chats but fails to produce post-call reflection prompts that guide the newcomer to recognize relationship depth and mentor potential on their own. The newcomer treats each coffee chat as a standalone event rather than a step in an ongoing relationship, and optimizes for volume over depth.

**R8 — Progress framing failure** *(new — identified post-evaluation)*
The navigator fails to reframe what "progress" looks like on a weekly basis. Without visible, relationship-based milestones (chats completed, relationships moving to a second meeting, things learned that couldn't be found on LinkedIn), newcomers measure success by job offers — which won't come for months — conclude networking isn't working, and quit before momentum builds. The fix is the third breakdown: the navigator actively surfaces week-over-week progress in terms newcomers can feel. If done well, this addresses the upstream problem (desperation mindset + expectation mismatch) without needing to treat it separately.

### why_user_validation

Specific assumptions only real users can clarify:

**On R1:**
- Do real newcomers actually have contacts with multiple connection types, or are most contacts cleanly one-dimensional?
- When a newcomer describes a contact, do they naturally surface both connection types — or do they lead with one and bury the other?

**On R7:**
- Do newcomers currently reflect on what happened in a coffee chat after the fact, or do they close the loop and move on?
- What signals, if any, do they already notice as "this was a good conversation"? Do they have any language for relationship depth?

**On R8:**
- How do real newcomers currently measure whether their networking is "working"?
- Can they articulate what "good progress" would feel like week by week — or are they so locked into "job offer = success" that the reframe needs to happen before anything else?
- What would they need to see or feel each week to stay motivated before results arrive?

## interview_plan

### target_users_for_research

Latino newcomers to Canada, under 5 years in the country, not yet working in their profession. Bilingual English/Spanish. At any stage of the networking journey — including those who haven't started yet.

### recruitment_approach

**Your Existing Network** (Lead Warmth: Hot | Setup Effort: Low)
Direct outreach to Latino newcomers Sandra has crossed paths with — former colleagues, community contacts, people met at events. Also includes referrals: asking existing contacts "who do you know that fits this profile?" One message to 5 people can surface 10 candidates.

Rationale: fastest path to conversations before the May 16 deadline; warmest leads reduce friction; authentic peer-to-peer dynamic fits the tool's tone.

### value_exchange

Early access to the tool when it's ready, with Sandra personally walking them through it. Authentic to the stage of the project and genuinely valuable to people who are still navigating the problem.

### outreach_message

> Hi [Name],
>
> I'm building an AI tool for Latino newcomers to Canada who are trying to break into their profession — and I'd love to learn from your experience.
>
> I'm at the research stage right now, which means no product yet — just genuine curiosity about what the journey actually looks like. I want to understand what's hard, what's confusing, and what you wish you'd had.
>
> Would you be open to a 30-minute conversation this week or next?
>
> In return, you'll get early access when the tool is ready — and I'll personally walk you through it.
>
> [Calendly link or "just reply and we'll find a time"]
>
> Sandra

### conversation_goals

- 5 interviews minimum before May 16
- 30 minutes per interview
- Mix of stages: some who've tried networking, some who haven't yet started
- Include 1-2 successful transitioners (now working in their profession) to surface what actually worked and capability gaps Sandra hasn't considered

## conversation_guide

### opening_warmup

*"Thanks so much for making time. Tell me a bit about your background — what did you do back home, and how long have you been in Canada?"*

Listen for: their profession, timeline, emotional tone when they describe the journey. This primes them to talk about their experience, not give abstract opinions.

---

### Track A — Still Navigating the Transition

Use for: newcomers not yet working in their profession.

**current_workflow_questions**

1. *"Walk me through what you've been doing to try to find work in your field since arriving. What does a typical week look like?"*

2. *"Have you tried networking — reaching out to people for coffee chats or informational interviews? What did that look like?"*

Listen for: whether they've tried networking at all, how structured vs. improvised it was, tools they use (LinkedIn, WhatsApp groups, nothing). If they haven't tried: ask why — that's an R8 signal.

**pain_point_questions**

3. *"Where does the job search process feel most overwhelming or stuck right now?"*

4. *"If you've had coffee chats — what part feels hardest? Getting them, preparing for them, or knowing what to do after?"*

5. *"When you put effort into networking and don't see results, what goes through your head?"*

Listen for: emotional language around desperation and timelines ("I've been doing this for months"), whether they measure progress by job offers or anything else (R8), whether they reflect on individual conversations or just move on (R7).

**current_solutions**

6. *"What have you tried so far to get better at networking or find connections? Any tools, groups, resources?"*

7. *"How's that working? What does it do well, and where does it fall short?"*

Listen for: newcomer programs (ACCES Employment, COSTI, settlement agencies), WhatsApp/Facebook groups, career coaches, mentors. This tells you what the navigator is competing against.

**ideal_outcome**

8. *"If you could change one thing about how you're approaching networking right now, what would it be?"*

9. *"What would make you feel like networking is actually working — before you land the job?"*

Question 9 directly tests R8. Listen for whether they can articulate a relationship-based progress signal, or whether job offer is the only metric they have.

---

### Track B — Successful Transitioners

Use for: newcomers now working in their profession in Canada.

**opening_warmup**

*"Tell me about your journey — what did you do back home, when did you arrive in Canada, and how did you end up where you are now?"*

Listen for: timeline, what finally shifted, whether networking played a role or something else did.

**current_workflow_questions**

1. *"At what point did things start to change? What were you doing differently?"*

2. *"How did networking fit into your search — did it actually lead to your role, or was it more indirect?"*

Listen for: the actual mechanism (referral, visibility, relationship that turned into an intro) vs. what they think worked. These often differ.

**pain_point_questions**

3. *"Before things clicked, what was the hardest part of networking for you?"*

4. *"Was there a moment where you almost gave up on networking? What kept you going?"*

Listen for: how they sustained motivation before results came, what progress felt like during that period (R8 signal).

**current_solutions**

5. *"What tools, programs, or resources did you use during your search? What actually helped vs. what was noise?"*

Listen for: things Sandra hasn't included in the solution, community resources, peer support mechanisms.

**ideal_outcome**

6. *"If you could go back and give yourself one thing that would have made the networking process easier or faster, what would it be?"*

7. *"Is there anything you learned about networking in Canada that surprised you — something you wish someone had told you earlier?"*

Listen for: cultural insights, specific tactics, mindset shifts, things not yet in the problem definition.

---

### closing

*"Is there anything about this experience I haven't asked that you think I should understand?"*

*"Who else do you know going through something similar that I should talk to?"*
